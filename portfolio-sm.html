Sustainability tackling smart mirror

The endless cycle of buying new clothes leaves closets full of unworn items, fueling a wasteful and unsustainable fashion industry.

Smart Mirror: A Prototype for Sustainable Wardrobe Management

Challenge

In today's consumer society, many people own an excess of clothing, much of which goes unworn. This not only contributes to environmental waste but also creates a personal challenge: the daily struggle of choosing an outfit from a cluttered closet. The goal was to design a system that could help users rediscover their existing wardrobe and foster more conscious consumption habits.

"Frequently people have way too many clothes, of which half of them they don't even wear. Not only is this very unsustainable, but it can also result in personal issues when selecting clothes from your closet."

Solution

The solution is a high-fidelity Smart Mirror prototype that uses NFC technology to bridge the gap between a physical wardrobe and a digital interface. By attaching small NFC tags to clothes, users can scan an item and instantly see outfit suggestions on the mirror. The system keeps a digital inventory, tracks how often items are worn, and can suggest donating underused pieces, encouraging a more sustainable and organized approach to personal style.


My role & tools

As the sole creator for this Human-Computer Interaction master's project, I was responsible for the entire design process from ideation to evaluation. This included:

  • Formulating the concept through brainstorming and MoSCoW analysis
  • Sketching low-fidelity concepts for the hardware and software
  • Designing with use of 3D modeling, e-textiles, electronics
  • Developing the interactive software and integrating it with the hardware
  • Conducting and analyzing user evaluation sessions

This project combined physical and digital prototyping to create a tangible user experience.


The process:
1. Concept & Low-fi Prototyping

The process began with brainstorming, mood boards, and sketching to visualize the user journey, hardware connections, and the core interface layout based on MoSCoW prioritization.

2. High-fi Hardware Prototyping

The physical components were built: a human figure and mirror frame were 3D modeled and printed. E-textiles were created by sewing NFC tags into miniature clothes. An Arduino Nano was connected to an RFID reader and LEDs to provide tangible feedback.

3. Software Development & Integration

An interactive interface was developed in Processing. This software received data from the Arduino when an NFC tag was scanned, displaying outfit options and responding to user clicks. The laptop's webcam was used to simulate the "mirror" effect, overlaying digital clothes on the user's reflection.

4. Evaluation

The complete prototype was evaluated with three participants. They used the "think aloud" technique while interacting with the system, followed by a post-session interview to gather qualitative feedback on usability, desirability, and potential for behavioral change.


Outcome & reflection

The evaluation showed that the prototype was a success. Key findings include:

  • The system was recognized for its potential to raise awareness about consumption and encourage more regular closet cleanouts.
  • Participants found the system intuitive and easy to use.
  • The concept was seen as a fun and useful tool for daily life.

Feedback confirmed the design's core value. While participants saw it as a "fun extra" rather than an essential "need," they positively acknowledged its potential to create a debate around fast fashion and change behavior. Future development could focus on adding accessibility features like voice commands and expanding the concept for use in retail environments.

Project overview

  • Project type Master coure Interaction Design (Academic Prototype Project)
  • Duration 2 months (December 2021 - January 2022)
